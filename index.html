150 years of Peterborough

Peterborough
Town

Click the audio player to listen to the story of Peterborough Town

Peterborough sits 244 kilometres north of Adelaide in South Australia's Mid North region. First settled in 1875 and originally named Petersburg after German landowner Peter Doecke, the town was renamed in 1918 as anti-German sentiment swept Australia during the First World War. Railway built this town. At its peak, over one hundred trains a day passed through Peterborough, connecting Broken Hill to Port Pirie and Adelaide to the outback. Three separate rail gauges met here, a distinction found nowhere else in Australia. The Steamtown Heritage Rail Centre, the ornate 1927 Town Hall, Bob the Railway Dog on Main Street, and the Social History Museum in the YMCA building all tell that story today. 150 years on, Peterborough endures.

Peterborough
high school

Peterborough High School is located 245 kilometres north of Adelaide in the Mid-North Region of South Australia. It has been at its current site since 1926, with the foundation stone laid in the original stone school building in September of that year.

The Cenotaph
and Rotunda

The rotunda was built in 1931 as a bandstand and war memorial, one of the few examples in South Australia built to provide work for the unemployed during the Great Depression. The twin pylon cenotaphs were unveiled in April 1934 by the Governor, Sir Alexander Hore-Ruthven, commemorating the 43 men from Peterborough killed in action during the First World War. Built in 1887

The Historic
Printing Works

At the prompting of mayor W. Thredgold, the Petersburg Times was founded in 1887 by Robert M. Osborne, became The Times and Northern Advertiser in 1919, and survived as a family business until 1970. The historic printing shop is a must-see, with many of the machines from a time when four separate newspapers were printed here in Peterborough, now carefully restored and back in operation for visitors. At its peak, the presses were as busy as the tracks outside.
